The honest answer is that this is less about which show is "best" and more about what kind of English problem you're actually trying to solve.

That said — **Friends**, and it's not particularly close.

---

Here's the thing. IELTS speaking isn't testing whether you can be funny, or whether you understand sarcasm, or whether you can follow a plot. It's testing something quite specific and, when you look at it closely, almost paradoxical: it wants you to sound natural while doing something deeply unnatural, which is _talking at length, coherently, about topics you were handed thirty seconds ago._

The skill that trains for that isn't wit. It's **texture** — the small connective tissue of everyday speech. How you buy time. How you walk back a claim. How you signal that you're about to make a point. And Friends, more than the other two, is essentially a masterclass in that texture.

---

**Breaking Bad**, for all its greatness, is the wrong register entirely. Walter White doesn't chat. He monologues, he manipulates, he delivers lines that were written to be remembered. The rhythms are cinematic, not conversational. You'd come away sounding like someone preparing a TED talk, which is not what a Part 2 examiner wants to hear.

**The Big Bang Theory** is trickier to dismiss, and to be fair, the argument for it has real force — it's dialogue-heavy, the characters explain things constantly, and the cadence is quick. But here's where it gets odd: the show's humor depends almost entirely on _register mismatch_. Sheldon is funny because he talks like a paper when everyone else is talking like a person. Spend enough time with that and you start internalizing the wrong lesson — that impressive English means formal English, which is almost the opposite of what the examiner is looking for.

Friends, by contrast, is six people doing almost nothing but talking to each other about ordinary things. Jobs, relationships, what happened yesterday, what might happen tomorrow. The topics are, loosely speaking, exactly the IELTS topic bank. And the language — _I mean, the thing is, I don't know, which is kind of the point, you know what I'm saying_ — is the connective tissue the examiner is listening for, whether they know it consciously or not.

---

What you're actually mining it for, specifically:

The **filler-that-isn't-filler** — _I mean_, _the thing is_, _honestly_, _it's funny you should ask_. These aren't empty sounds. They're buying time while sounding fluent, which is exactly the skill Part 2 is testing.

The **self-correction rhythm** — characters change their minds mid-sentence constantly. _I think — actually no, the better way to put it is._ That pattern, absorbed naturally, is worth more than any list of transition phrases.

The **topic pivot** — Friends characters move between topics the way IELTS part three moves between topics: _okay but speaking of which_ / _which reminds me_ / _that's kind of what I was saying about_. The examiner wants to see you navigate. This is what navigating sounds like.

---

So there's that. Not the funniest answer, probably not the one you were hoping for, but make of that what you will — Friends is the one, and it's worth sitting with the slightly deflating reason why: the show that will help you most is the one where, when you really look at it, not very much happens.
