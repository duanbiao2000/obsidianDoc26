**The Principle: Preference Reversal** _(using a new illustration)_

---

A river merchant named Dao kept two boats. The first was a sturdy old vessel — reliable, known, guaranteed to complete any journey but slow and costly to maintain. The second was a sleek newer craft — faster, cheaper to run, but with a hull that had never been tested in the storm season. When the river guild asked Dao which boat he valued more for his trade, he answered without hesitation: the newer boat. It was the future of his business, he said. Any fool could see it.

When the guild's assessor arrived to set insurance bonds — asking Dao to name the sum he would demand to surrender each boat permanently — something shifted beneath Dao's reasoning like silt in slow water. For the old boat, he named a high figure. It had never failed him. Losing it felt concrete, visceral, like losing a limb. For the newer boat, he named a modest sum — its promise was still hypothetical, its value somehow harder to hold in his hands. When a flood forced him to sacrifice one boat to save the dock, he surrendered the newer one. The numbers he had spoken aloud had quietly made the decision for him. He gave up the boat he claimed to love more.

The guild assessor recorded no contradiction. The merchant himself felt none. Yet the village elder, watching from the bank, understood what had happened. The same man had answered two different questions — _which do you prefer_ and _what would you take to give it up_ — and the river had revealed that these were not the same question at all. Dao had not lied. He had simply discovered that what a creature reaches for when choosing and what it demands when bargaining are governed by entirely different currents, running in opposite directions beneath the same surface.

This is **preference reversal** — documented by Lichtenstein and Slovic, later foundational to behavioural economics — in which agents consistently rank option A above option B yet assign higher monetary valuations to B under separate pricing tasks. Classical economics assumes preferences are stable and method-independent: how you ask should not change what you want. The fable shows otherwise. Dao's _choice_ (the newer boat) and his _pricing_ (the older boat valued higher) cannot both be true expressions of a single coherent preference — yet both emerged from the same man, on the same morning, in perfect sincerity. The implication is that preference is not a fixed internal object waiting to be retrieved, but something partially constructed by the act of measurement itself.

> "This is actually something that's stuck with me for a while, so I'm glad I have a chance to talk about it.

The other was a corporate role — stable, well-paid, but honestly a bit soul-crushing from the description alone. And I was completely clear in my head: I wanted the creative job. I told everyone. I was almost evangelical about it.
>

And the moment I started thinking in numbers rather than feelings, something flipped. The corporate salary suddenly felt concrete and loseable in a way the creative opportunity didn't — because the creative job's upside was still hypothetical. So I ended up valuing the corporate role more highly in my own calculations.
>
> I took the corporate job. And the strange thing is, I didn't feel like I'd contradicted myself — it happened so gradually. It was only months later, when I was sitting in a beige meeting room at 9am on a Monday, that I thought — wait. I actually chose the thing I said I didn't want.
>
> Looking back, I think the way I was asked to evaluate the options completely changed what I valued. Ranking them felt like one question; pricing them felt like another. And apparently those two questions don't always live in the same part of your brain."

---

**Part 3 — 学术讨论**

**Q: Do you think people generally know what they actually want?**

> "I used to assume yes — that preferences are just sitting there inside us, waiting to be expressed. But I've become genuinely skeptical of that. There's pretty compelling evidence that what people say they prefer and what they actually choose can be systematically inconsistent, depending entirely on _how_ you ask them. Which suggests that preference isn't really a fixed internal state — it's partially constructed in the moment of measurement. That has fairly uncomfortable implications, not just for individuals, but for things like consumer research, policy design, even democratic choice — if how you frame the question changes the answer, who's really doing the deciding?"

**Q: Should governments make certain decisions for people, given that people aren't always rational?**

> "It's a genuinely difficult tension. The paternalist case is strong — if people's stated preferences are unreliable, then nudging them toward objectively better outcomes seems defensible. But there's a real danger in assuming that whoever designs the nudge has more accurate access to people's 'true' preferences than the people themselves do. I think the more honest position is that rationality is contextual — people aren't globally irrational, they're locally inconsistent in predictable ways. The policy response should be to improve decision environments, not to substitute someone else's judgment wholesale."

## Version 3 — 雅思写作高分范文

**预测题目：**

> _Some people believe that individuals always know what is best for themselves and should be free to make their own choices. Others argue that people often make irrational decisions and therefore need guidance from governments or experts._
>
> _Discuss both views and give your own opinion._

---

**范文（约290词，Task 2学术类）：**

The question of whether individuals can reliably identify their own best interests sits at the intersection of economics, psychology, and political philosophy. While personal autonomy is a cornerstone of liberal democratic societies, mounting evidence suggests that human decision-making is systematically inconsistent in ways that complicate a straightforward defence of unfettered choice.

The case for individual freedom rests on a principle of epistemic privilege: each person has unique access to their own values, circumstances, and priorities that no external authority can fully replicate. Overriding personal choice, even with benign intent, risks substituting one fallible judgment for another while simultaneously eroding the dignity and agency that make human life meaningful. History offers ample warning of what happens when governments presume to know better — from paternalistic welfare policies that entrapped rather than liberated, to ideological regimes that systematically discounted individual experience in favour of collective abstraction.

Yet the assumption that stated preferences accurately reflect underlying interests is increasingly difficult to sustain. Behavioural research consistently demonstrates that individuals rank options differently depending solely on how choices are presented — a phenomenon that exposes preference not as a stable internal fact, but as something partially constructed by the act of choosing itself. If the mechanism of measurement shapes the outcome, the notion of a singular, retrievable 'true preference' becomes philosophically suspect. This does not, however, straightforwardly justify governmental intervention. The more defensible response is to improve decision environments — through transparent information, carefully designed defaults, and removal of artificial urgency — rather than to replace individual judgment with institutional authority.

In conclusion, the dichotomy between free choice and guided decision-making is less a binary than a spectrum. People are not uniformly irrational; they are predictably inconsistent in specific contexts. The most intellectually honest policy position acknowledges this complexity and responds with structural support rather than substitution of autonomy.
