## 🗣️ Part 1 — 自然流畅（7.5+）

**Q: Did you enjoy school?**

> _"For the most part, yes. But looking back, I think there was too much pressure on exam results and not enough focus on wellbeing. I remember feeling quite stressed during exam season — I think most students did."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

**Q: What was your favourite subject?**

> _"Probably science, because it felt relevant to the real world. I think that's what makes a subject engaging — when you can see why it matters beyond the classroom."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

**Q: Do you think education is important?**

> _"Absolutely. There's a quote from the video I recently watched — 'if you want to see the economy of the future, look at the classrooms of today.' I think that really captures it."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

## 🗣️ Part 2 — 描述卡（Cue Card）

**Describe a teacher or school that influenced you.**

> _"I'd like to talk about my primary school teacher, who genuinely changed the way I think about learning._
>
> _She had this ability to nurture each student's potential — she never made you feel like you weren't good enough. What stood out was that she didn't just focus on academic results. She cared about whether we were confident, curious, and happy._
>
> _Looking back, I think that's what holistic education really means — it's not just about preparing students for exams, but equipping them for life. Research actually shows that countries with the best education systems prioritise teacher quality above everything else, and she was a perfect example of why._
>
> _She influenced me because she made me believe that effort matters more than natural ability — and that's a mindset I've carried into my career."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

## 🗣️ Part 3 — 深度观点（7.5+）

**Q: Why do some countries have better education systems than others?**

> _"I think it comes down to political will and long-term investment. Countries like Singapore and Estonia made education a national priority from the start — they didn't have natural resources, so developing human capital was the only path forward. What's interesting is that it's not always about money — some nations spend a lot but still underperform, so it really comes down to how wisely resources are used."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

**Q: Do you think standardised tests are a fair way to measure ability?**

> _"Not entirely. Tests like Pisa are useful for benchmarking countries against each other, but they only measure three subjects — reading, maths and science. They completely miss things like creativity, resilience, and emotional intelligence. So while they're a helpful snapshot, they don't tell the whole story."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

**Q: Is it the government's responsibility to ensure equal education for all?**

> _"I'd say yes, primarily. The gap between what wealthy and disadvantaged students achieve is deeply unfair — and individuals can't fix that on their own. Government policy is the lever that can actually move the dial. That said, families and communities also play a role — research shows parental involvement has a big impact on outcomes, regardless of income level."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

**Q: Do you think too much pressure is put on students today?**

> _"In many countries, definitely. There's growing evidence that excessive academic pressure harms students' wellbeing without necessarily improving their performance. Some of the highest-achieving systems in Asia, for instance, are starting to question whether the cost — in terms of stress and burnout — is worth it. Balance is key."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyn5xWvx44)

## 🗣️ Part 1 — 自然流畅（7.5+）

**Q: Do you like watching documentaries?**

> _"Yes, quite a lot. I recently watched one about Chernobyl — it was made forty years after the disaster, and what struck me was hearing from people who were actually there. It felt much more real than reading about it in a textbook."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

**Q: Are you interested in history?**

> _"I am, especially when it connects to the present. The Chernobyl story is a good example — it's not just history, because the site is still radioactive today, and it even became a battleground again during Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

**Q: Do you trust the news?**

> _"Mostly, but I think you have to be critical. The Chernobyl disaster is a classic example of how governments can suppress information — Soviet authorities tried to cover it up for days before the truth came out."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

## 🗣️ Part 2 — 描述卡（Cue Card）

**Describe an important historical event you learned about.**

> _"I'd like to talk about the Chernobyl nuclear disaster, which happened on April 26th, 1986 — forty years ago this month._
>
> _I learned about it through a BBC documentary. What made it so striking was hearing firsthand accounts from people who lived in Pripyat, the nearby city. One couple had literally just got married the night of the explosion — their first dance as a couple happened as the reactor was becoming radioactive. That detail really stayed with me._
>
> _In terms of impact, the consequences were catastrophic and far-reaching. The explosion contaminated vast areas of three countries and forced hundreds of thousands of people to evacuate permanently. Perhaps most remarkably, the Soviet government attempted a cover-up — they delayed telling the public the truth, which put even more lives at risk._
>
> _I think what we can learn from Chernobyl is the danger of prioritising reputation over transparency. When governments hide the truth in a crisis, the human cost is always much higher."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

## 🗣️ Part 3 — 深度观点（7.5+）

**Q: What can governments learn from major disasters like Chernobyl?**

> _"The biggest lesson is transparency. In Chernobyl's case, the Soviet authorities tried to downplay what had happened, which delayed the evacuation and exposed far more people to radiation than necessary. A modern government facing a crisis needs to communicate clearly and quickly — hiding the truth always makes things worse in the long run."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

**Q: Do you think nuclear energy is safe?**

> _"It's a complex question. Disasters like Chernobyl show the catastrophic potential when things go wrong. But it's also worth noting that Chernobyl was partly the result of a flawed reactor design and a culture of secrecy, rather than nuclear power being inherently dangerous. Modern reactors have much stronger safety protocols, so I think the risk-benefit calculation has shifted."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

**Q: How do major disasters affect society in the long term?**

> _"The effects can be enormous and last for generations. Chernobyl is a perfect example — the contamination is still there forty years on, the nearby city of Pripyat remains abandoned, and the psychological impact on survivors has never fully healed. On a political level, some historians argue it actually hastened the collapse of the Soviet Union, because it exposed how deeply the system had failed its own people."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)

**Q: Should governments always be transparent with citizens during a crisis?**

> _"Absolutely, I think transparency is essential. When authorities withhold information, people can't make informed decisions to protect themselves. The liquidators at Chernobyl — the workers sent to clean up the disaster — were often not fully told of the dangers they faced, and many paid with their health or their lives. That's an unacceptable cost of keeping secrets."_[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=f5Z-1DfEtAM)
